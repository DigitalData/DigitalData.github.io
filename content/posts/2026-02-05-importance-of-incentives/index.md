> “Show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome.”

― Charlie Munger

*I assume I am one among many who believe in the implication of this quote. I would personally argue that most work done by humans can be directly or indirectly attributed to a set of incentives and disincentives.*

## What do I consider incentives?
It's always important to define terms when speaking generally to avoid confusion.

When I talk about an incentive, I think in terms of what how a behaviour/act is perceived punished or rewarded. I specify "perceived" because this is not always explicit. An incentive can be corollary ("when I do this, then this tends to happen, and that's good/bad") or causal ("When I do this, it causes this to happen"). Essentially, note that correlation does not necessarily indicate causation, and that we humans interpret these effects as such nonetheless.

The clearest example of a type of incentive is financial incentives. AKA gaining some money. 

A disincentive is specifically a subset of perceived punishment for actions and behaviours.

## Incentives can produce negative outcomes.
In a society where the average member trusts that others will abide by societal expectations, those who are not trustworthy have an advantage.

The expectation for society is that everyone has agreed to a contract: They will display certain behaviours and avoid others in exchange for everyone else in the society doing the same.

For the purposes of my thought experiment, the rules of the social contract don't necessarily need to be defined. 

Instead, I focus on someone who chooses to buck the terms of the contract. If they see an advantage in ignoring part of the contract (i.e. if their subversive behaviour yields benefit), then they can engage in the subversive behaviour until they are caught / punished for it.

If they are caught, then society may punish them in accordance with the social contract.
Until they are caught, or if there is no punishment in the contract, only the expectation that a behaviour is not adopted, then these subversive actors have an incentive to continue the behaviour.



## So incentives are ever-present. Why should they matter?
Simply, I am concerned that we do not do any of the following enough:
- considering how we are incentivised to repetitive actions/behaviours.
- using incentives to our advantage.
- being mindful of the incentives we create for others.

### Do you reflect on the incentives that drive your decisions?
We are driven by incentives in our daily lives, whether we like it or not. 
- I bought a pack of chips because of the positive experience from the flavour. The dopamine hit is an incentive.
- I got out of bed today (a work day) because despite not being particularly enthused to have a job, I am both rewarded for doing so, and punished if I don't (or at least that is what I believe). My rewards are income. My punishment for inaction would be the conversation with my manager for missing a day, and the loss of said income.
- I choose to value my income because it allows me to eventually afford to purchase my own property that I can use for my purposes. A corollary relationship between income and freedom of expression/choice/action presents itself here.

### Manage your incentives, you will manage your outcomes.
Create a system that works best for you, but I highly recommend using the incentives that work best on you to motivate you to achieve your goals.
- Quitting smoking. I do not know what it is like to be a smoker, but I imagine one way to minimize incentives to continue is to normalize other forms of breaks in your day. Grabbing a coffee, going for a walk, or my personal favourite, finding a place to sit and make some progress on an online course. The gut reaction is that these acts feel like a scam on your employer or time, but they stand to yield more gains for everyone than the accepted act of the smoko.
- Inducing productivity. Boredom is such a great disincentive for doing nothing. I find that my points of greatest, most positive (not just working for the sake of working) productivity at work happen just after I reach points of extreme boredom. I have now learned to accept this sinusoidal rhythm in my job.
