I am not an economist, nor am I someone you should trust.
However, I have found myself chafing at the current policies adopted
with respect to alleviating real estate cost concerns.
It is not a secret that real estate is now becoming a 
significant portion of income for renters and borrowers.
So instead of moping, I thought I should throw some ideas out there.

## Flexible and hybrid working
Demand for real estate tends to be unevenly distributed, 
with concentrations around cities and working areas.
This trend is primarily influenced by:
- The locus of work
- The locus of community

So let me step on my soap box and support a greater push for flexible and hybrid working. Policies that support hybrid/flexible working would reduce pressures on households to be close to city centers, allowing for wider distribution of demand towards the outskirts (or even away from) cities. A friend of mine with whom I worked at a previous role lives over an hour away from the city/office, and I believe that a contributing factor to the purchase of his house was the flexible working arrangement that he was able to negotiate for himself. Policies should aim to normalize, or potentially enshrine a right to hybrid working for those who do not need consistent contact hours. 

As a software engineer, I do not need to be in the office very often. My team is globally distributed and most meetings are conducted via video call anyways. This is likely the truth for many others working in distributed teams (regionally, or globally).

The return to office in the post-COVID world was likely more of a function of financial commitments over optimizing performance. I can understand the need to make your money's worth on an office lease, but to what extent do all these employees need to make a physical appearance on the daily?

## Support for regional hotspots
As mentioned above, work and community play a large part in determining where home-seekers will set their sights. 
While modern developing nation-states focus on developing key cities to attract business and innovation, we see in developed cities issues of concentration. We are at an age of unprecedented ability to coordinate and communicate, so why not harness this to expand past the idea of the city for innovation, and make it a state-wide focus?

Policies to achieve this could include developing or incentivizing the development of key community hubs around a region, instead of placing them all within one city. The next big mall or high-rise licensing package could receive favorable terms based on a less developed area that may be seeking an infusion of community-building. 

## Reliable and accessible public infrastructure
Before you ask, yes, I am quite interventionist in my thinking.
Infrastructure such as roads, but more importantly, *good public transport* can contribute to alleviating a need for proximity to central hubs.

Speaking personally, one of my key requirements when looking for property is to be near a train station. I would even go so far as to say that in Melbourne, where I reside, trains are the most reliable form of public transit. Buses and trams would not make an area more desirable to me. I also believe that a solid road network can also reduce the perceived time-cost for long-distance commuters.
