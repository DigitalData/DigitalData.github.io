> “Show me the incentive and I'll show you the outcome.”

― Charlie Munger

I assume I am among many who believe in the implication of this quote. I would personally argue that most work done by humans can be directly or indirectly attributed to a set of incentives and disincentives.

## What do I consider incentives?
It's always important to define terms when speaking generally to avoid confusion.

When I talk about an incentive, I usually think about something that is gained from some act. Some words that I would loosely correlate to incentives include "perks" or "benefits".

The clearest example of a type of incentive is financial incentives. AKA gaining some money. 

A disincentive would be like an incentive to not engage in an act.

## "Direct"? "Indirect"?
I think there are incentives that we can consider direct because the connection to a sense of personal benefit is clear to see. 
More money can afford a better quality of life.

An indirect incentive would usually be harder to spot. As I write this my mind goes to systems built around promoting dopamine production in the brain. Uses of bright colours or celebratory language to stimulate a brain to view something (a product, for example) in a more positive light. I would consider this an incentive because the brain subconsciously makes a connection between the incentivized action and a positive gain.

## So what? This is pretty widely known stuff.
Yes, and I am concerned that we do not do any of the following enough:
- Being mindful of the influence of incentives on us.
- Using incentives to our advantage.
- Being mindful of the incentives we create for others.

### Do you reflect on the incentives that drive your decisions?
We are driven by incentives in our daily lives, whether we like it or not. 
- I bought a pack of chips because of the positive experience from the flavour. The incentive is the positive experience.
- I went to work because I did not want to deal with the conversation with my manager that would happen. I also went to work because I know that I will receive an income that I can use to spend on myself. The awkward conversation is a disincentive to stop working, and the income is an incentive to work.
- I see smokers on the street daily, falling for the incentives of having a socially-accepted break from work, a drug-induced positive experience, and to prevent withdrawal.

The mere process of identifying all of these incentives at play in our daily actions helps put things into perspective. It can also help address acts that we do not want to continue.

### Manage your incentives, you will manage your outcomes.
Create a system that works best for you, but I highly recommend using the incentives that work best on you to motivate you to achieve your goals.
- Quitting smoking. I do not know what it is like to be a smoker, but I imagine one way to minimize incentives to continue is to normalize other forms of breaks in your day. Grabbing a coffee, going for a walk, or my personal favourite, finding a place to sit and make some progress on an online course. The gut reaction is that these acts feel like a scam on your employer or time, but they stand to yield more gains for everyone than the accepted act of the smoko.
- Inducing productivity. Boredom is such a great disincentive for doing nothing. I find that my points of greatest, most positive (not just working for the sake of working) productivity at work happen just after I reach points of extreme boredom. I have now learned to accept this sinusoidal rhythm in my job.

- Is it the only incentive to work? **No.**
- Would people still work without this incentive in place? **I would argue that the concept of *work* would look very different to how it looks now.**
